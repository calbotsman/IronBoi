# Strategic Gut-Check: MYO Coach Personas and Graphics-and-Gameplay UX

- **Author:** Rowan (Strategist)
- **Date:** 2026-06-08

## a) Strongest Version: How Personas Serve the Wedge

The anxious-to-returning lifter feels lost because they don't trust their own judgment — they need a coach who explains *why* and remembers *them*. Public-figure personas can serve this wedge if framed as **explanatory authority**, not celebrity worship. For example, "train like Huberman" becomes "Huberman's protocols explained for your goals," where the persona is a lens for *why* certain choices matter. This taps into the user's desire for credible guidance without overwhelming them with jargon or complexity. The persona becomes a shorthand for trust ("Huberman knows this stuff") and a scaffold for understanding ("here's why you're doing this"). Done right, it reinforces the wedge by providing both credibility and clarity.

## b) Strongest Version: How Personas Weaken the Wedge

The wedge is about *personalized coaching* for the anxious lifter, not *celebrity mimicry*. Public-figure personas risk fragmenting the product into a **cult of personality**, where users pick personas based on fandom rather than fitness needs. This could alienate the anxious lifter, who already feels out of place in gym-bro culture. Worse, it could introduce cognitive load ("which persona is right for me?") and undermine the trust MYO is trying to build ("why am I following Huberman's protocol instead of a plan tailored to me?"). If personas feel like a novelty feature rather than a core coaching mechanism, they dilute the wedge by making MYO feel less like a personal coach and more like a branded playlist.

## c) The Call: Ship Personas, But Frame Them as Explanatory Lenses

Ship personas, but frame them as **explanatory lenses**, not celebrity endorsements. Position them as "ways to understand your training" rather than "ways to train like a celebrity." The copy should emphasize *why* the persona's approach is relevant to the user's goals ("Huberman's protocols for recovery" or "Mentzer's intensity principles for strength"). This keeps the focus on the user's needs while leveraging the persona's credibility. Avoid celebrity worship — the persona is a teaching tool, not a hero to emulate.

## d) Positioning Logic for "No Financial Lead" + Graphics/Gameplay UX

The "no financial lead" + graphics/gameplay UX positions MYO as a **trust-building tool**, not a transactional product. Duolingo succeeds here by making the free experience feel complete — users pay to enhance, not unlock, core functionality. MYO can follow this logic by framing monetization as "premium features for advanced users" rather than "paywalls for basic access." For example, free users get personalized coaching with one persona; premium unlocks multiple personas, advanced analytics, or deeper customization. The graphics/gameplay UX reinforces this by making the free experience feel rich and engaging, so users pay to deepen their engagement, not to access it.

## e) Positioning Risks to Manage Before Shipping

1. **Risk of Celebrity Worship:** Users might see personas as "train like a celebrity" rather than "train with credible guidance." Solution: Frame personas as explanatory lenses, not hero worship.
2. **Risk of Fragmentation:** Users might feel overwhelmed by persona choice, undermining the personalized coaching wedge. Solution: Limit initial personas to 2-3 and guide users toward the most relevant option.
3. **Risk of Feeling Transactional:** The graphics/gameplay UX might feel hollow if users sense paywalls lurking. Solution: Make the free experience feel complete, with premium features framed as enhancements, not unlocks.
